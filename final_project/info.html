Information on all Things Cannabis

General Cannabis information

Cannabis, commonly known as marijuana, is a versatile plant that has been used for various purposes throughout history. It belongs to the Cannabaceae family and comprises three primary species: Cannabis sativa, Cannabis indica, and Cannabis ruderalis. The plant contains a diverse range of chemical compounds, including cannabinoids, terpenes, and flavonoids, which contribute to its unique properties. While cannabis has a long history of recreational and medicinal use, it has gained increasing attention in recent years for its therapeutic potential. The two most well-known cannabinoids found in cannabis are THC (tetrahydrocannabinol), known for its psychoactive effects, and CBD (cannabidiol), recognized for its potential medicinal benefits. Cannabis can be consumed in various forms, such as smoking, vaporizing, edibles, and extracts, and its legal status varies widely across different regions and countries.

THC vs CBD

THC (Tetrahydrocannabinol):

  • Psychoactive Effects: THC is the primary psychoactive compound in cannabis, responsible for the "high" or euphoric sensation that users experience.
  • Binding to CB1 Receptors: THC binds primarily to the CB1 receptors in the central nervous system, producing its psychoactive effects.
  • Effects on Cognition: THC can affect cognitive functions such as memory, concentration, and coordination.
  • Medical Uses: THC has therapeutic properties and is used medically for pain relief, nausea reduction (especially in cancer patients undergoing chemotherapy), and stimulation of appetite (commonly in HIV/AIDS patients).

CBD (Cannabidiol):

  • Non-Psychoactive: Unlike THC, CBD does not produce a psychoactive "high." It does not bind strongly to CB1 receptors, which are mainly concentrated in the brain.
  • Interaction with CB2 Receptors: CBD interacts with CB2 receptors found in the peripheral nervous system and immune cells, contributing to its potential anti-inflammatory and immune-modulating effects.
  • Anxiolytic and Antipsychotic Properties: CBD is known for its potential anxiolytic (anxiety-reducing) and antipsychotic properties, making it appealing for those seeking therapeutic benefits without the intoxicating effects.
  • Medical Uses: CBD has been explored for various medical purposes, including the treatment of epilepsy (Epidiolex, a CBD-based medication, is approved for certain seizure disorders), anxiety, inflammation, and pain management.

Types of THC cannabinoids

Below is a table of some key differences in different variants of THC

Delta 6

  • Delta-6 THC is a less common and less researched variant of THC
  • It has a double bond at the 6th carbon atom in the molecular chain

Delta 7

  • Delta-7 THC is another less common variant
  • It has a double bond at the 7th carbon atom

Delta 8

  • Delta-8 THC is an analog of Delta-9 THC with a double bond at the 8th carbon atom
  • It is psychoactive but generally considered less potent than Delta-9 THC

Delta 9

  • Delta-9 THC is the most well-known and abundant form of THC in cannabis
  • It has a double bond at the 9th carbon atom and is responsible for the psychoactive effects associated with cannabis

Delta 10

  • Delta-10 THC is a less common and less researched THC variant
  • It has a double bond at the 10th carbon atom

Delta 11

  • Delta-11 THC is another variant of THC, with a double bond at the 11th carbon atom
  • Limited research is available on Delta-11 THC, and its effects are not well-documented

THC-A (Tetrahydrocannabinolic Acid):

  • THC-A is the acidic precursor to THC found in raw, unheated cannabis
  • THC-A needs to undergo decarboxylation (heating) to convert into THC, the psychoactive form

THC-P (Tetrahydrocannabiphorol):

  • THC-P is a relatively newly discovered cannabinoid; it is reported to be more potent than Delta-9 THC, with potential effects lasting longer
  • Attaches to CB1 receptors better then some otehr cannabinoid giving it these possibilty of higher potency

THC-B (Tetrahydrocannabivarin):

  • THC-B is a non-psychoactive cannabinoid with structural similarities to THC
  • It is distinct from THC and THC-A in its effects and properties

THC-V (Tetrahydrocannabivarin):

  • Naturally occurring cannabinoid found in certain cannabis strains
  • It has a reputation for producing a more uplifting and energetic high compared to THC

Sativa, Hybrid, or Indica

Sativa

Sativa strains are often chosen for activities that require mental alertness and physical activity. Some users prefer Sativa for addressing mood disorders and fatigue.

  • Energizing and uplifting
  • Can enhance creativity and focus
  • Often associated with daytime use

Hybrid

Hybrids offer a combination of the effects associated with both Sativa and Indica strains. Users often choose hybrids based on their desired balance of effects.

  • Effects can vary widely depending on the specific hybrid and its parent strains
  • Hybrids can be balanced (50/50) or lean more towards Sativa or Indica dominance

Indica

Indica strains are often chosen for relaxation, stress relief, and sleep. They may be beneficial for managing pain and muscle spasms.

  • Relaxing and sedating
  • Can induce a "body high" and promote sleep
  • Often associated with evening or nighttime use

Methods of using Cannabis

Below is a table of different methods of using cannabis

Smoking

Method: Dried cannabis flowers are smoked in a pipe, joint, or bowl

Advantages

  • Quick onset of effects
  • Precise control over dosage

Disadvantages

  • Inhalation of smoke may pose health risks
  • Shorter duration of effects compared to some other methods

Vaporizing (Vaping):

Method: Cannabis is heated to a temperature that releases cannabinoids and terpenes without combustion

Advantages

  • Reduced exposure to harmful substances found in smoke
  • Potential for more controlled dosing

Disadvantages

  • Initial cost of a vaporizer device
  • Higher learning curve on vaporizing flower correctly

Edibles:

Method: Cannabis-infused into food products, such as brownies, gummies, or beverages

Advantages

  • No inhalation, reducing harm to the respiratory system
  • Long-lasting effects

Disadvantages

  • Slower onset of effects (may take 30 minutes to 2 hours)
  • Dosage can be challenging to control

Tinctures:

Method: Liquid cannabis extract (alcohol or glycerin-based) placed under the tongue for sublingual absorption

Advantages

  • Quick onset of effects
  • Precise dosing

Disadvantages

  • Alcohol-based tinctures may have a strong taste
  • Taste can linger in mouth for some strong tasting tinctures

Dabbing:

Method: Cannabis concentrates (wax, shatter) are vaporized on a hot surface and inhaled

Advantages

  • High potency
  • Quick onset of effects

Disadvantages

  • Requires specialized equipment
  • High potency may not be suitable for novice users

Topicals:

Method: Cannabis-infused lotions, balms, or patches applied to the skin

Advantages

  • No psychoactive effects
  • Targeted relief for localized issues (pain, inflammation)

Disadvantages

  • Limited systemic effects
  • Can be hard for some with disabilities to apply topical in needed location
